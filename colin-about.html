01
Where I'm from

Raised on the Bay, the mountains,
and a Giants game.

I grew up in the East Bay — the kind of place where you can be at the beach in the morning and deep in the Sierras by afternoon. That access to both city and wilderness shaped how I see the world. I learned early that the best experiences happen when you leave the comfortable and go find the interesting.

I bled orange and black at Giants games, lost my voice at Warriors playoff runs, and stayed loyal to the St. Mary's Gaels through thick and thin. The Bay gave me community, competition, and an early sense that geography shapes character — and I carry that with me everywhere.

"The Bay gave me access to everything — and taught me to never take for granted where you're standing."
02
Why engineering

I was taking things apart before I could explain why.

Almost before I could walk, I had a TV remote in my hands. It didn't take long before I was pulling them apart — not to break them, but to understand them. That compulsion to see inside a thing, figure out what makes it tick, and imagine how it could work differently has never left me.

When it came time to choose a path, engineering wasn't a career choice — it was the closest word for what I already was. Human-Centered Engineering at Boston College caught my eye because it tied a rigorous technical degree to something bigger: Boston College's Jesuit mission of becoming what the world needs you to be. Engineering in service of people, not just systems.

"I didn't choose engineering. I just finally found a name for what I'd always been doing."
03
The outdoors

The trail taught me things no classroom could.

I grew up hiking with my family and spending summers in the Sierras. There's something about carrying everything you need on your back that strips a problem down to its essentials — weight, weather, route, risk. I fell in love with backpacking in high school and never looked back.

When I got to BC, finding the Outdoor Adventure Club was one of the first things I did. I became a trip leader, planning and guiding multi-day backpacking trips for 6+ students at a time — handling logistics, gear, meal planning, and the kind of real-time problem solving that no case study can replicate.

Running came later — first for fitness, then for the challenge of it. I've worked up through longer distances and find that the same discipline that gets you through a long training run translates pretty directly to everything else worth doing.

04
What drives me

Solar energy is the problem of our generation.

I'm drawn to the energy sector — and solar in particular — because the window for meaningful impact is wide open. The technology is maturing fast, efficiency breakthroughs are still being made, and demand is accelerating. This is the moment to be building in this space.

I took Solar Engineering at BC and came away more energized than ever. The intersection of materials science, systems thinking, and global-scale impact is exactly the kind of problem I want to spend my career on. Not just as an engineer inside a company — eventually as a founder building something of my own.

The throughline from disassembling a TV remote at age four to wanting to build a solar energy company isn't a straight line. But it's the same instinct: find something broken, understand it completely, and build it better.
